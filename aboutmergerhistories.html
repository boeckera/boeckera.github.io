Disentangling the merger history of galaxies

Image Credit : CFHT, Coelum, MegaCam, J.-C. Cuillandre (CFHT) & G. A. Anselmi (Coelum); Original Website

Galaxies grow in stellar mass by either star formation or the accretion of smaller galaxies. Sometimes the disruption of satellite galaxies during the merger process is visible on sky as faint streams or shells, such is the case for NGC 474 on the left. While these features are indicative of recent accretion events, mergers that happened in the early epochs of the universe are very well phase mixed and no longer visible in the surface brightness.

I therefore investigated and developed an alternative method, which exploits the fact that galaxies of different masses undergo a different chemical enrichment, therefore their origin is still imprinted in their stellar populations properties. By measuring the age-metallicity distribution of a galaxy (blue shaded in panel a) and overlapping average chemical evolution templates for galaxies of different masses (color-coded lines), we can estimate how much fractional mass could have come from accreted satellite galaxies (panels b and c). I successfully tested this method with simulated galaxies from EAGLE .

For more details have a look at my publication as well as a first appliation of this method to observational data .
